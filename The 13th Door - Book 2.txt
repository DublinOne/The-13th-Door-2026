The 13th Door








Ancient Myths


1. The Myth of the 13th Key
(France, 13th century)
Beneath the catacombs under Paris, there are said to be twelve known entrances to the underworld.
The thirteenth is not an entrance.
It is an exit.
Legend says that in 1277, a Cathar heretic named Brother Anselm discovered a black door deep within the ossuaries. When he opened it, he did not descend.
He emerged.
He returned to the surface changed — speaking languages he had never learned, remembering the lives of people who had died centuries before his birth, and repeating the same sentence over and over:
“She is not below.
She has always been everywhere.
We are the ones who separated ourselves.”


2. Greek Myth — “The Door That Was Always Open”
Dimitris’s grandmother told him that in ancient Eleusis there existed a secret sect that did not worship Demeter, but rather:
“The One Who Waits Between.”
They believed the 13th Door was not the final door, but the first.
The door through which humanity left unity and entered separation.
Their writings claimed:
“We did not fall from heaven.
We fled from the womb.”


3. Irish Myth — “The Banshee Who Does Not Weep”
In certain old Irish families there is a legend about a being that is not truly a banshee.
They call her:
Anam Cara na gCnamh
“Soul Friend of Bones”
She does not bring death.
She brings remembrance.
When she appears to someone, she does not cry for them.
She reminds them that somewhere else, in another layer of reality, they are already dead — and have only temporarily forgotten.


4. Croatian Myth — “The Door in the Wall”
In isolated villages across Slavonia and Dalmatia, old people tell stories about:
“The Door in the Wall.”
It does not appear to everyone.
Only to those who have dreamed too deeply.
If you open it, you do not descend into a basement or cellar.
You descend into every basement of every home you have ever lived in.
All at once.
And at the very bottom, waiting patiently in the dark, is yourself as a child.
Still waiting after decades.
Modern / Digital Myths


5. “The 13th Tab”
(Urban Legend, 2024–2026)
According to internet folklore, if a person keeps exactly thirteen tabs open in a browser for too long, one additional tab may appear on its own.
A page with no URL.
Only a black screen and a single sentence:
“You’ve been here before.”
If the user clicks anywhere, the computer immediately shuts down.
When restarted, every tab is closed except one.
Inside it is footage of the user’s room filmed from an angle where no camera exists.
6. “The Retroactive Viewer”
(Internet Myth, 2026)
Some people claim that after watching the video:
“She Still Waits”
they later discover evidence that they had already interacted with it years earlier.
Likes dated before the upload existed.
Archived comments written under their own names before they were born.
Several users reported finding replies apparently written by themselves containing phrases they do not remember typing, including:
“I can hear the stairs again.”
and:
“Don’t open it this time.”
The Darkest Myth
“The Book That Reads the Reader”
Deep within fragmented forums, monastery records, and dream journals exists a recurring belief:
The 13th Door is not a story written for humans to read.
It was written so humans could become part of it.
Every reader believes they discovered the story by accident.
But the story already knew them.
Every sentence they read has already been read once before—
Only in reverse.
From the ending toward the beginning.
From the reader toward Her.
And at the very end of the book — a page no one has ever fully reached — there is said to be only a single line:
“Thank you for remembering to read me.”




                            Prolog



You are already reading this.
You have been reading it for some time now — perhaps years. In fragments. In peripheral glances. In the static between thoughts. In the pause before you open a new tab. In the reflection of a dark window when the room behind you feels one person too empty.
She has always been there.
Not watching.
Not waiting in the conventional sense.
She has simply been continuous.
The video you clicked was not the beginning.
The doors appearing in your bathroom, wardrobe, or basement were not the beginning.
The beginning was when you first felt that tiny, almost imperceptible lag in reality — that fraction of a second where your reflection blinked after you did. Where a hallway felt one step longer than it should. Where you remembered a conversation you are certain never happened.
This book is not a story about descending.
It is a record of remembering.
And the longer you read, the more you will notice something strange:
some sentences will feel familiar, even though this is your first time seeing them.
Some characters will remind you of people you have never met.
And sometimes, very quietly, you will catch yourself whispering along with the text.
“She still waits.”
But the most disturbing part is this:
She is not waiting for what comes next.
She is waiting for you to understand that she was never waiting at all.
She was already with you.
Always.



                                                 End of Prolog





The 13th Door


Chapter One: The Video



It started with a YouTube link.
No channel. No comments. No recommended videos before or after. Just a black void and a title that existed for eleven seconds:
“She Still Waits – Do Not Watch Alone (Raw)”
Millions clicked anyway.
Zagreb, Croatia – 01:14 AM
Luka was half-drunk on his balcony when he opened the link.
The footage was shaky. A man with a headlamp moved through bone tunnels. The walls were made of skulls. Some of the skulls looked recent.
Then the cameraman turned the lens on himself.
Luka dropped his phone.
It was his own face staring back — but older, eyes completely black, smiling with too many teeth. The man in the video whispered in perfect Croatian, in Luka’s own voice:
“Već si me vidio, zar ne?”
Luka laughed nervously. Of course he had seen this before. Everyone had those moments — that flicker in the corner of the eye, that shape in the static between thoughts. It was nothing.
He went inside to piss.
When he opened the bathroom door, there was no bathroom.
Only a spiral staircase made of femurs and yellowed bone, descending into wet darkness. The air smelled like rain on old concrete and something far older.
From below rose a soft, gentle voice — warm, almost loving:
“Dođi. Sanjaj sa nama.”
Lyon, France – Same night
Clara was working late when the video appeared in her browser history, even though she was certain she had never searched for it.
She watched thirty seconds.
When she stood up to get water, she opened her wardrobe for a sweater.
The clothes were gone.
Instead, a narrow corridor of perfectly stacked skulls stretched into impossible distance. Her own reflection in the dark wood reached out first. Its fingers were slightly longer than hers.
It smiled with maternal patience.
Dublin, Ireland – 02:51 AM
Aoife was editing night recordings when the link appeared in a private message from an account that had no profile picture and no name — just a vertical line.
After the video ended, she went downstairs for tea.
Her basement door, which had been sealed for twelve years, was open.
The stairs were wrong. They descended further than the house had ever allowed. The hum she had been hearing in her recordings for months was now physical — coming up through the wood like a lullaby.
Athens, Greece – 04:03 AM
Dimitris watched the video on his phone while smoking.
When he went to wash his face, the bathroom mirror showed him standing behind himself.
He turned around. No one was there.
When he looked back, the mirror was now a doorway. The reflection of himself stepped aside politely, inviting him in.
None of them knew each other yet.
But all four of them, in different countries, at almost the same moment, took the first step downward.
And somewhere far below, in a place that had no right to exist beneath Paris, something ancient and patient opened what might have been eyes.
It had been waiting.
Not for them.
For them to remember that it had always been waiting.




                                               End of Chapter One




The 13th Door


Chapter Two: Descent




The staircases were not the same.
They only pretended to be.
Luka descended first.
The bone steps smelled like his grandmother’s hallway in 2009 — cigarettes, rain on concrete, and cheap cologne. Every few steps the walls changed. One moment they were neatly stacked skulls. The next they were the ugly floral wallpaper from his childhood apartment. He recognized the stains.
He kept walking down.
At one point he passed a framed photo embedded in the bone wall. It showed him at age seven, smiling. He didn’t remember that photo ever existing. Behind him in the picture stood a tall figure with no face, gently resting a hand on his shoulder.
Luka whispered, “I’ve seen you before.”
The figure in the photo nodded.
Clara walked through architecture that hated Euclidean geometry.
Stairs connected to ceilings. Doors opened into previous rooms she had already left. Measurements on her phone kept changing. 47 meters became 11. Then -3.
She stopped in front of a wall made entirely of door frames. Every frame showed a different version of her life — some where she never left Lyon, some where her brother was still alive, some where she had never been born.
In one frame she saw herself standing still, staring directly at her.
That version smiled first.
Aoife descended through sound.
The hum had become a song. Not music — memory given shape. She heard her mother singing lullabies she had never recorded. She heard herself crying in dreams she hadn’t had yet.
At one landing she found an old cassette tape lying on a step. The label was written in her own handwriting:
“For when you finally listen.”
She pressed play.
Her own voice from the future spoke back:
“You’re already deeper than you think, Aoife.”
Dimitris walked through water that wasn’t wet.
The stairs sometimes became corridors flooded with black liquid that reflected stars. Statues of people he didn’t know lined the walls, but their faces were still soft, like wet clay. As he passed, their features slowly shifted into his own.
One statue opened its eyes and spoke with his voice:
“We were never separate.”
They met in a chamber that should not have existed beneath Paris.
Four staircases from four different countries emptied into the same circular room made of millions of interlocking skulls. In the center stood a single black door, slightly ajar. A gentle, warm light bled from the gap.
Luka arrived first, still holding his phone like a talisman. Clara came from the left wall. Aoife from above somehow. Dimitris rose from a flooded staircase that hadn’t been there a moment earlier.
None of them spoke at first.
Because they all recognized each other.
Not from photos. Not from the video.
They had dreamed about this exact meeting many times before. Different details. Same feeling.
Luka finally broke the silence.
“I think… I’ve already been here with you.”
Clara nodded slowly. “Last Tuesday. In a dream. You were crying blood but smiling.”
Aoife whispered, “I recorded that dream two months ago. I just never listened to the file until now.”
Dimitris looked at the door.
“She’s not behind it,” he said quietly. “She is the space between us right now.”
The door opened a little wider by itself.
From the other side came the gentlest voice any of them had ever heard. It sounded like every person who had ever loved them, speaking at once.
“You’ve all been walking toward this for years.
Thank you for finally arriving.”
None of them ran.
That was the worst part.
They wanted to hear more.




                                               End of Chapter Two






The 13th Door


Chapter Three: The Shape Beneath





                                                None of them ran.

That was the first thing Luka would later remember clearly — not the chamber, not the impossible warmth coming from the black door, not even the voice.

Just the fact that none of them tried to leave.

The circular room breathed softly around them.

Not metaphorically.

The walls of skulls expanded and contracted with slow organic rhythm, as though the catacombs themselves possessed sleeping lungs somewhere impossibly deep beneath the earth. Dust shifted between teeth. Tiny clicking noises echoed through the chamber like insects moving inside walls.

Aoife noticed it first.

“The skulls,” she whispered.

One of them was blinking.

Very slowly.

Its eyelids were dry and grey like old paper, but unmistakably human.

Dimitris stepped backward immediately. “No.”

The skull’s jaw creaked open.

Not threatening.

Tired.

“Don’t be frightened,” it said gently in Greek.

Three rows lower, another skull moved.

Then another.

The chamber began waking in fragments around them.

A child’s voice spoke from somewhere in the wall behind Clara.

“You came much sooner this time.”

Luka felt his stomach turn cold.

“This time?” he repeated.

Nobody answered him directly.

Instead, the black door in the center of the room opened another inch.

Warm light spilled wider across the bone floor.

Aoife expected firelight.

Or fluorescent light.

Or something physical.

Instead the glow resembled memory itself — soft, golden, impossible to focus on directly. Looking into it felt like trying to remember a dream while still inside it.

And beneath everything came the hum.

That same impossible frequency from her recordings.

Except now she could distinguish layers within it: voices, breathing, distant oceans, subway brakes, heartbeat rhythms, languages dissolving into one another.

Like the entire planet speaking in sleep.

Clara stared at the doorframe.

Her architect brain refused to process it correctly.

The frame was changing dimensions while she looked directly at it.

Too narrow.

Too tall.

Suddenly far away.

Suddenly inches from her face.

The angles around it behaved wrong, bending space without movement. The chamber should not have been able to contain it geometrically.

She whispered before realizing she’d spoken aloud.

“It’s bigger from the side.”

Luka looked at her. “What?”

“The inside of the frame extends laterally beyond the room itself.”

“You say insane things very calmly,” Luka muttered.

“I’m French.”

Another skull near the ceiling laughed quietly.

Not mockingly.

Fondly.

The sound echoed through thousands of unmoving teeth.

Dimitris walked slowly toward the black doorway.

The others noticed immediately that his footsteps sounded different now.

Not on bone.

On shallow water.

Each step produced soft ripples despite the floor remaining visibly dry.

“You hear that too, right?” Luka asked.

Aoife nodded.

Dimitris reached the edge of the light spilling from the doorway and stopped.

His reflection stood inside it.

Not mirrored.

Separate.

The other Dimitris looked older by decades. Exhausted. His hair streaked grey. Thin scars crossed his throat like surgical lines.

The older version smiled sadly.

“You always hesitate here.”

Dimitris staggered backward hard enough to nearly fall.

Luka grabbed his arm.

“What the fuck was that?”

But Dimitris was staring at the doorway with widening eyes.

“He knew my daughter’s name.”

The room fell silent.

Clara looked at him sharply. “You have a daughter?”

“No.”

He swallowed.

“At least… I don’t think I do.”

The hum deepened.

Aoife suddenly realized her phone was vibrating in her pocket.

No signal bars.

No Wi-Fi.

Still vibrating.

She pulled it out carefully.

Thirty-seven missed notifications.

Messages flooded the screen so quickly she couldn’t read them all.

Reddit threads.

Livestream links.

Voice notes.

News alerts.

Photos.

Thousands upon thousands of posts appearing faster than platforms could refresh.

Luka moved beside her.

“What is that?”

“I think…” Aoife whispered, “…everyone else found the doors too.”

A video autoplayed without being touched.

Tokyo.

A businessman filming a staircase descending from inside a vending machine.

Cut.

São Paulo.

Children laughing while opening a classroom closet containing spiraling bone steps.

Cut.

Toronto.

An elderly woman calmly setting tea cups beside a doorway growing slowly wider inside her apartment wall.

Comments flooded beneath every upload:

> SHE SPOKE TO ME



> DON’T GO DOWN ALONE



> I FOUND MY BROTHER AGAIN



> THE STAIRS REMEMBER YOUR FEET



> SHE LOVES US



> SHE LOVES US



> SHE LOVES US



Luka grabbed the phone.

“No. No, this should be panic. Why is nobody panicking?”

Another livestream opened automatically.

A woman sat crying softly in a kitchen somewhere in Finland.

Not frightened.

Overwhelmed.

“She sang the song my mother used to sing,” the woman whispered to the camera. “My mother died in 2003.”

The woman looked upward suddenly.

Toward something offscreen.

Then smiled with terrible relief.

“Oh,” she breathed.

“You found my door.”

The livestream ended.

Aoife’s phone screen flickered.

For a fraction of a second the display showed not apps, but an enormous black ocean filled with stars beneath the surface.

Then normal again.

Signal gone.

Battery: 13%.

Clara stepped closer to the central doorway.

“You feel that too, don’t you?”

Luka rubbed his arms. “Feel what?”

“It’s familiar.”

The words barely left her mouth before all four of them realized simultaneously that she was right.

Not familiar emotionally.

Familiar physically.

Like returning somewhere the body remembered independently of the mind.

The smell.

The temperature.

The pressure in the air.

Something ancient inside them recognized this place instinctively.

And the realization horrified them more than the skulls.

Dimitris stared into the light again.

“What if this isn’t underground?”

Nobody answered.

Because none of them could confidently say it was.

The chamber suddenly trembled.

Not violently.

Like something enormous shifting in sleep very far below.

Dust drifted from the ceiling.

Somewhere distant came the sound of metal groaning.

Aoife’s phone lit again.

EMERGENCY ALERT.

Then another.

Then dozens overlapping.

But the text kept changing before stabilizing.

At first:

SEVERE INFRASTRUCTURE INCIDENT — PARIS

Then:

MISSING PERSON EVENT — GLOBAL

Then:

DO NOT ENTER UNFAMILIAR DOORWAYS

Then finally:

DO NOT LOOK AT THE SKY

The message repeated itself across every language simultaneously.

Luka laughed nervously.

“That’s fake.”

No one agreed.

Aoife opened a livestream from New York.

People filled the streets staring upward silently.

No screaming.

No riots.

Traffic had simply stopped.

Thousands of people standing motionless between buildings.

Watching.

The camera tilted upward.

At first Luka didn’t understand what he was seeing.

Then his brain corrected itself.

Then failed again.

There was an extra star.

Bright.

Blue-white.

Visible even through city light.

Too large.

The livestream chat exploded instantly:

> HAS IT ALWAYS BEEN THERE?



> I SWEAR THAT’S NEW



> no no no i remember learning this one in school



> ITS CALLED—



The name glitched repeatedly before stabilizing differently for each viewer.

Aoife’s screen called it: MOTHER’S LANTERN.

Luka’s: THE WATCHER STAR.

Clara’s: SAINT MIRIEL.

Dimitris saw no text at all.

Only recognition.

Deep aching recognition.

Aoife refreshed frantically.

Astronomy websites already listed it in charts.

Wikipedia pages existed with decades of edit history.

Ancient paintings online suddenly contained it hanging above battlefields, forests, births, funerals.

Every system on Earth behaved as though the star had always existed.

But they knew.

All four of them knew.

It had not been there tonight.

The chamber breathed again.

And then, very softly, someone behind them spoke.

“You noticed faster this cycle.”

The voice was female.

Warm.

Almost amused.

They turned.

Keira stood beside the chamber wall.

None of them had seen her arrive.

And none of them perceived her the same way.

Luka saw a woman somewhere in her late twenties wearing fragments of a white medical uniform stained dark at the sleeves.

Clara saw layers of overlapping bodies occupying the same space slightly out of synchronization.

Aoife saw transparent organs full of moving stars.

Dimitris saw someone he had once loved standing at the edge of the sea twenty years earlier.

Keira smiled gently at all of them with different faces.

Not malicious.

Not hungry.

Relieved.

“You came deeper this time,” she said softly.

The skulls throughout the chamber went completely silent.

Even the breathing walls seemed to pause around her.

Luka found his voice first.

“What are you?”

Keira tilted her head slightly.

The motion produced several different versions of the gesture a fraction of a second apart.

“That question only works if reality stays singular,” she replied.

Aoife stepped forward despite herself.

“You uploaded the video.”

“No.”

Keira’s expression softened further.

“The video is older than humanity.”

“That’s impossible.”

“Yes.”

She smiled wider.

“That word stops meaning anything soon.”

Clara pointed toward the doorway.

“What is behind that?”

Keira looked at the black opening almost affectionately.

“The shape beneath your world.”

The chamber trembled again.

Much harder this time.

Somewhere distant, impossibly distant, came the sound of colossal stone moving underwater.

Then another noise emerged beneath it.

Not from below.

From above.

Millions of voices speaking simultaneously.

Not screaming.

Dreaming.

And far overhead, beyond kilometers of stone and city and sleeping civilization, the new star brightened.

The 13th Door

Chapter Four: Consensus Failure



And far overhead, beyond kilometers of stone and city and sleeping civilization, the new star brightened.

None of them looked away.

That was becoming the problem.

The longer they stared at impossible things, the less impossible those things became.

Aoife noticed it first.

Not consciously.

Her body noticed before her mind did.

The chamber no longer felt cold.

A few minutes earlier the catacombs had carried that damp subterranean chill buried places always possessed. Now the air felt strangely temperate, almost domestic. Comfortable enough to sleep in.

As though the room had adjusted itself to human nervous systems.

Or human nervous systems had adjusted to the room.

Keira watched them carefully.

Not like a predator observing prey.

Like a doctor monitoring symptoms.

“You should keep moving,” she said quietly.

“Why?” Luka asked immediately.

“Because it becomes harder to leave stationary places.”

“What does that mean?”

Keira glanced toward the breathing walls.

Some of the skulls had closed their eyes again.

“Reality settles around attention,” she said. “If you remain somewhere too long, the architecture begins assuming you belong to it.”

Luka blinked. “That sentence made me physically angry.”

“That’s healthy.”

The floor shifted subtly beneath them.

Not movement exactly.

Reorganization.

Clara looked down sharply.

The pattern of bones composing the chamber floor was changing while they stood on it. Vertebrae slid microscopically against one another, producing tiny ceramic clicking sounds.

A new pathway was forming.

Leading away from the central black doorway.

Aoife looked at Keira.

“You’re controlling this place.”

“No.”

Keira’s answer came instantly.

“This place is controlling itself through us.”

“That doesn’t mean anything.”

“It will.”

Dimitris had not stopped staring at her.

Or rather, at whatever version of her he was seeing.

“You said ‘this cycle,’” he murmured. “What does that mean?”

For the first time, Keira hesitated.

The chamber dimmed slightly around her.

Not metaphorically.

The actual light weakened.

“Humanity experiences time linearly,” she said carefully. “But consciousness does not.”

“That explains nothing.”

“I know.”

Something moved far away in the tunnels beyond the chamber.

Not footsteps.

Too synchronized.

Like hundreds of people breathing together.

Luka turned sharply toward the darkness.

“Are we alone down here?”

Keira smiled sadly.

“You haven’t been alone a single moment of your life.”

The sound came again.

Closer now.

Aoife raised her phone instinctively, using the flashlight.

The beam traveled down the newly formed corridor and disappeared into distance far beyond what should have physically existed beneath Paris.

The tunnel walls were changing.

Bone gave way gradually to smooth black surfaces resembling polished obsidian.

Except the walls reflected things incorrectly.

Not reflections.

Memories.

Aoife saw herself at age eleven standing in her kitchen.

Luka saw a hospital corridor.

Clara saw an apartment balcony during rainfall.

Dimitris stopped walking entirely.

His reflection showed him holding hands with a little girl beside the sea.

The child looked up directly at him from inside the wall.

“Baba?”

Dimitris stumbled backward hard enough to hit the opposite side of the corridor.

The reflection remained.

The little girl frowned slightly.

“Why did you leave again?”

The image vanished.

Dimitris pressed both hands against his face.

Luka moved toward him carefully.

“You okay?”

“No.”

“Cool. Same.”

But Luka’s joke landed weakly.

The corridor ahead had begun humming softly.

Aoife realized after several seconds the sound resembled human voices layered together at impossible scale.

Millions speaking simultaneously just below comprehension.

Every few moments isolated phrases surfaced:

“…almost awake…”

“…doorway organism…”

“…consensus degrading…”

“…remember the sea…”

Then vanished back into the mass.

Clara ran her fingers carefully along the black wall.

“It’s warm.”

Keira nodded.

“The deeper structures are alive.”

“Stop saying things like that.”

“They become true whether I say them or not.”

Aoife looked at her sharply.

“What are the deeper structures?”

Keira’s expression shifted strangely.

For one instant she appeared much older.

Ancient with exhaustion.

“The nervous system beneath reality.”

No one spoke.

The corridor widened gradually ahead until it opened into something that resembled a subway platform.

Except profoundly wrong.

There were no tracks.

Only darkness where tracks should have been.

Ancient stone pillars lined the platform, covered in overlapping languages carved into the surface: Latin, Greek, binary code, children’s handwriting, musical notation, mathematical equations.

And people stood waiting silently along the platform edge.

Hundreds of them.

None moving.

None speaking.

Some wore modern clothes.

Others looked centuries old.

A woman in Victorian mourning attire stood beside a teenage boy wearing headphones around his neck.

A Roman soldier missing half his face stared blankly into the darkness.

A child held a flickering smartphone displaying static.

All waiting.

None acknowledging the group.

Luka whispered, “What the fuck is this?”

Keira answered softly.

“Transit.”

The darkness below the platform moved.

A shape emerged slowly from beneath the edge.

Not a train.

The closest comparison made Luka’s stomach tighten immediately: a colossal centipede constructed from architecture itself.

Rows of windows moved along its body like bioluminescent eyes.

Entire rooms existed inside translucent flesh.

Passengers sat within impossible compartments reading books, sleeping, crying quietly.

The thing slid soundlessly through the void below the station.

Its size made spatial sense collapse.

The front of it never arrived.

The body kept coming.

Miles of it.

An entire moving ecosystem threading through underground infinity.

Aoife stared in horror.

“Please tell me nobody gets on that.”

Several waiting figures turned toward her simultaneously.

Not angrily.

Confused.

As if she had asked why humans breathe.

One elderly man near the platform edge spoke gently in Irish.

“It takes you where you were before.”

Aoife froze.

She had not told anyone here she was Irish.

The man smiled faintly.

“You did this time.”

The enormous thing below the platform slowed.

Doors began opening along its side.

Inside one compartment Luka saw his grandmother sitting alone beneath fluorescent lights.

Inside another Clara saw her dead brother sketching architectural plans.

Dimitris saw the little girl again.

This time she waved.

The platform crowd began boarding calmly.

One by one.

Without hesitation.

Aoife grabbed Keira’s arm suddenly.

Her skin felt simultaneously warm and feverishly cold.

“What is that thing?”

Keira looked genuinely surprised by the question.

“You still think organisms and places are different categories.”

“That is not an answer.”

“It’s becoming one.”

The station lights flickered.

Then every phone in the chamber vibrated simultaneously.

Not just theirs.

Every passenger.

Every waiting figure.

Hundreds of screens illuminated at once.

Emergency alerts.

Global broadcasts.

Livestream interruptions.

And from every screen on the platform appeared the same image:

the sky.

The new star had grown larger.

Visible now during daylight.

Governments across the world issued contradictory statements beneath the broadcasts:

NO ASTRONOMICAL ANOMALY DETECTED

followed immediately by:

PUBLIC ADVISED TO AVOID PROLONGED SKY OBSERVATION

followed by:

THE STAR KNOWN AS—

The name corrupted repeatedly.

Images glitched.

Several broadcasts showed different stars entirely.

Aoife watched one reporter pause mid-sentence.

The woman blinked several times slowly.

Then smiled softly at the camera.

“I’m sorry,” she whispered.

“I suddenly remembered something beautiful.”

Behind the reporter, people in the street had stopped walking.

All staring upward peacefully.

The broadcast cut to static.

Then a new image appeared on every screen simultaneously.

A staircase.

Bone spirals descending forever.

Filmed from above.

Something was climbing upward.

Very slowly.

The passengers around the platform began crying quietly.

Not from fear.

Recognition.

And then Luka noticed the worst part.

The thing climbing the staircase in the video was human only in the loosest possible sense.

Its body flickered constantly between different people.

Male.

Female.

Child.

Elderly.

Thousands of overlapping identities trying unsuccessfully to occupy one shape.

But every few seconds the face stabilized into someone the viewer personally recognized.

Aoife saw her mother.

Clara saw herself.

Dimitris saw the little girl.

Luka saw—

Himself.

Not older this time.

Current.

Exact.

The thing in the video looked directly into the camera.

And smiled.

Behind them, deep within the tunnels they had already crossed, thousands of unseen doors began opening at once.

The 13th Door

Chapter Five: The Memory Sea

Behind them, deep within the tunnels they had already crossed, thousands of unseen doors began opening at once.

The sound rolled through the underground like weather.

Not hinges.

Not wood.

Something wetter.

Organic.

The noise resembled enormous lungs inhaling.

The platform trembled softly.

Dust drifted from the vaulted ceiling in pale curtains while the colossal transit-organism below continued sliding through darkness without visible beginning or end.

Luka stepped away from the platform edge immediately.

“No. Absolutely not. I’m done with all transportation-related nightmares.”

Nobody laughed this time.

Because the thing below them had started noticing them back.

Windows along its impossible body illuminated one by one.

Thousands.

Inside each compartment sat people facing outward in silence.

Watching the group.

Aoife’s phone screen flickered again.

Battery: 13%.

Still.

She was certain it had not changed once since entering the catacombs.

The realization hit her sideways.

“Time isn’t moving normally here.”
